# Training Module: Behavioral Training

> Hero: **Address Common Behavioral Challenges** — Evidence-based solutions for the
> most common behavioral issues dog dads face. These challenges are normal and
> fixable with patience and consistency. (Page offers "Download Module PDF".)

**Before You Begin** — Behavioral issues often have underlying causes. Rule out medical issues first with your vet. Pain, illness, or hormonal changes can cause sudden behavioral changes. If a behavior is severe, rapidly worsening, or involves aggression toward people, please consult a certified professional dog trainer (CPDT) or veterinary behaviorist (DACVB).

## Excessive Barking

Barking is natural communication for dogs, but excessive barking can be problematic. Understanding why your dog barks is the first step to addressing it.

**Common Causes:** alert/territorial barking at people or animals passing by; attention-seeking when they want something; boredom or lack of mental/physical stimulation; anxiety or fear-based responses; excitement or play arousal.

**Training Solutions:**

1. **Identify the Trigger** — Spend a few days noting when and why your dog barks. Is it at the doorbell? When left alone? At other dogs?
   1. Keep a barking diary for 3-5 days
   2. Note the time, location, and what triggered it
   3. Look for patterns in the behavior
2. **Remove or Manage Triggers** — For territorial barking at windows, close curtains or block the view. For doorbell barking, consider temporarily silencing the doorbell while training.
   1. Block visual access to common triggers
   2. Use white noise to mask outside sounds
   3. Create a calm environment during training
3. **Teach 'Quiet' Command** — Let your dog bark 2-3 times, then hold a treat near their nose (they must stop barking to sniff it). Say 'Quiet,' wait 2-3 seconds of silence, then reward. Gradually increase quiet time.
   1. Wait for natural barking to occur
   2. Use a treat to interrupt the barking
   3. Say 'Quiet' only once
   4. Wait for 2-3 seconds of silence
   5. Mark and reward the quiet behavior
   6. Gradually increase the duration of quiet
4. **Provide Adequate Exercise** — A tired dog is a quiet dog. Many barking issues stem from pent-up energy and boredom.
   1. Ensure 30-60 minutes of exercise daily (varies by breed)
   2. Add mental stimulation with puzzle toys
   3. Practice training exercises daily
5. **Don't Reward Barking** — Even negative attention rewards barking. If your dog barks for attention and you yell at them, you've given them what they wanted - your attention.
   1. Ignore attention-seeking barking completely
   2. Only give attention when they're quiet
   3. Turn your back and avoid eye contact during barking
   4. Reward quiet behavior frequently

**What NOT to Do:**
- Yelling at your dog - they think you're barking with them
- Shock or citronella collars - these don't address the root cause
- Comforting a dog who's barking from anxiety - this rewards the behavior
- Expecting immediate results - behavior change takes weeks

## Separation Anxiety

True separation anxiety goes beyond normal missing you - it's a panic disorder that causes extreme distress when alone. Signs include destructive behavior, excessive vocalization, and house soiling only when you're gone.

**Common Causes:** sudden change in schedule or living situation; loss of a family member or another pet; traumatic event when alone; never learned to be comfortable alone; over-attachment to one person.

**Training Solutions:**

1. **Desensitization to Departure Cues** — Dogs with separation anxiety often panic when they see signs you're leaving (grabbing keys, putting on shoes). Desensitize them to these cues.
   1. Pick up your keys randomly throughout the day, then set them down
   2. Put on shoes, sit on the couch for 10 minutes, then take them off
   3. Touch the door handle multiple times without leaving
   4. Practice these cues 10-20 times daily until they stop causing anxiety
2. **Gradual Alone Time** — Start with very short absences (30 seconds) and gradually build up. Never comfort them when anxious - wait for calm before giving attention.
   1. Start by leaving the room for 10-30 seconds
   2. Return before they show anxiety, reward calmness
   3. Gradually increase to 1 minute, then 2, then 5
   4. Move to stepping outside for 30 seconds
   5. Slowly build to 10, 20, 30 minutes over several weeks
   6. If they show anxiety, you've gone too fast - go back a step
3. **Create Positive Associations** — Make being alone a good thing. Special treats or toys that only come out when you leave.
   1. Get a puzzle toy or Kong and stuff it with high-value treats
   2. Give it to them only when you leave
   3. Take it away when you return
   4. This should be the best treat they get all day
   5. They'll start looking forward to you leaving
4. **Establish a Calm Departure Routine** — Don't make a big deal about leaving or returning. Long goodbyes increase anxiety.
   1. Give the special toy/Kong
   2. Calmly say 'I'll be back' in a matter-of-fact tone
   3. Leave without dramatic farewells
   4. When you return, ignore them for 5-10 minutes
   5. Greet them calmly once they've settled
5. **Provide Adequate Exercise** — Exercise before leaving helps them rest while you're gone rather than pacing with anxiety.
   1. Walk or play for 30-45 minutes before departures
   2. Include mental stimulation (training, sniff walks)
   3. Let them settle for 15-20 minutes after exercise
   4. Then provide the special departure treat
6. **Consider Professional Help** — Severe separation anxiety may require a veterinary behaviorist and potentially anti-anxiety medication.
   1. If your dog is injuring themselves, see a vet immediately
   2. Destructive behavior or hours of vocalization may need medication
   3. A certified animal behaviorist can create a custom plan
   4. Medication + training is often more effective than training alone

**What NOT to Do:**
- Getting another dog to 'keep them company' - it usually doesn't help
- Crating dogs with severe anxiety - they may injure themselves trying to escape
- Punishing destruction that happened while you were gone
- Rushing the process - this can take months of patient work

## Jumping on People

Dogs jump to greet faces and get attention. It works - we usually give them attention (even if negative). To stop jumping, we must make it unrewarding and teach an alternative behavior.

**Common Causes:** seeking attention and interaction; excitement and over-arousal; learned behavior - it worked before; lack of impulse control training; not enough physical exercise.

**Training Solutions:**

1. **Remove the Reward** — Jumping works because it gets attention. Remove all attention (positive or negative) when jumping occurs.
   1. The moment paws leave the ground, turn your back
   2. Cross your arms and look at the ceiling
   3. Stay completely silent - no eye contact, touch, or talk
   4. Wait for four paws on the floor
   5. The instant all paws are down, turn and calmly pet/greet
   6. If they jump again, turn away immediately
2. **Teach an Alternative Behavior** — Dogs need to do something when greeting. Teach them to sit for greetings instead.
   1. Before opening the door, ask for a sit
   2. Only open the door when sitting
   3. If they break the sit, close the door and reset
   4. When you enter, if they jump, turn away
   5. The moment they sit, reward with attention and treats
   6. Practice this 10-20 times per day
3. **Practice With Helpers** — The hardest part is greeting guests. Set up training sessions with friends who will follow the rules.
   1. Brief your helper on the protocol before they arrive
   2. Have them knock, you ask your dog to sit
   3. Open door only when dog is sitting
   4. If dog jumps on guest, guest turns away completely
   5. Guest only pets when four paws are down
   6. Repeat with multiple different people
4. **Manage Excitement Levels** — Some dogs are so excited they can't think. Lower the arousal before greetings.
   1. Exercise before guests arrive
   2. Practice calm behaviors for 10 minutes before the doorbell
   3. Keep greetings calm - no high-pitched exciting voices
   4. Consider having your dog on leash for greeting practice
   5. Reward calm behavior heavily
5. **Be Consistent** — Everyone must follow the same rules, every single time. One person allowing jumping undoes all your work.
   1. Brief everyone who interacts with your dog
   2. Post a sign on your door reminding guests
   3. Never allow jumping - not even when you're in 'play clothes'
   4. Remember: you're teaching that jumping NEVER works

**What NOT to Do:**
- Kneeing them in the chest - this can injure and increase excitement
- Holding their paws - they see this as interaction/reward
- Yelling 'No!' or 'Down!' - still attention, still rewarding
- Allowing jumping sometimes but not others - very confusing
- Grabbing their collar aggressively - can cause defensive behavior

## Resource Guarding

Resource guarding is when a dog protects valuable items (food, toys, bones, even people) by growling, snapping, or biting. This is serious and may require professional help, but early stages can be addressed at home.

**Common Causes:** natural instinct to protect valuable resources; history of having things taken away; competition from other pets; lack of trust that good things will come back; fear of scarcity.

**Training Solutions:**

1. **Never Punish Guarding** — Growling is communication. If you punish it, they may skip the warning and go straight to biting. We want to change their emotional response, not suppress warning signs.
   1. If they growl, back away - don't escalate
   2. Never take things away while they're guarding
   3. Thank them for the warning (they're trying to communicate)
   4. Work on building positive associations instead
2. **Trade, Don't Take** — Teach your dog that giving up items results in something even better coming back.
   1. When they have a toy, approach and offer a high-value treat
   2. Say 'Trade' and hold out the treat
   3. When they drop the toy to take the treat, mark with 'Yes!'
   4. Give them several treats
   5. Give the toy back
   6. Repeat daily with various items
   7. They'll learn that giving things up means good things happen
3. **Add Value When Approaching** — Change their emotion from 'Uh oh, they're coming to take my stuff' to 'Yay! Someone's coming to make it better!'
   1. When your dog has a toy or chew, walk past them
   2. Toss a high-value treat near them without stopping
   3. Keep walking
   4. Repeat multiple times a day
   5. Gradually get closer to dropping the treat
   6. Eventually drop treats right next to their valued item
   7. Over time, approach = good things arrive
4. **Practice 'Drop It'** — Teach a positive 'drop it' cue using toys, not high-value resources.
   1. Play tug with a toy
   2. Hold the toy still and stop playing
   3. Hold a treat to their nose - they'll drop the toy
   4. Say 'Drop it' as they release
   5. Give the treat, then give the toy back
   6. Practice until they drop on cue without showing the treat
   7. Gradually work up to higher-value items
5. **Manage the Environment** — While training, prevent situations that trigger guarding.
   1. Feed in a quiet area away from other pets/kids
   2. Put away high-value chews when not supervised
   3. Teach children to never approach a dog with food/toys
   4. If multiple dogs, feed separately
   5. Use baby gates to create safe spaces
6. **When to Get Professional Help** — Some cases require a certified behaviorist or veterinary behaviorist.
   1. If they've ever bitten or drawn blood
   2. If they guard you from family members
   3. If the guarding is getting worse despite training
   4. If you feel unsafe or unsure
   5. If children are in the home - get help immediately

**What NOT to Do:**
- Taking things away forcefully - this confirms their fear
- Punishing growling - removes their warning system
- Practicing on high-value items too soon
- Allowing children to approach while eating/chewing
- Using alpha rolls or dominance techniques - these are dangerous

## Dog-Dog Socialization Issues

Not all dogs need to be friends with every dog, but they should be able to calmly exist around other dogs. Poor socialization can lead to reactivity, fear, or aggression.

**Common Causes:** missed critical socialization period (3-14 weeks); traumatic experience with another dog; lack of positive dog interactions; over-arousal and poor impulse control; fearful or anxious temperament.

**Training Solutions:**

1. **Start at a Distance** — Find your dog's threshold - the distance where they notice other dogs but aren't reacting. This is where training happens.
   1. Go to a park or area where you can see dogs from far away
   2. Note when your dog first notices another dog
   3. If they're calm, mark and reward heavily
   4. Stay at this distance for several sessions
   5. Gradually decrease distance over weeks/months
   6. Always stay below threshold - if they react, you're too close
2. **Teach 'Look at That' Game** — Teach your dog that seeing other dogs = treats from you. This builds positive associations.
   1. When your dog notices another dog, immediately mark and treat
   2. Repeat: see dog = treats
   3. After many repetitions, they'll look at the dog, then back at you for treats
   4. This is called 'auto-check in'
   5. Reward the check-in heavily
   6. Practice at increasing levels of distraction
3. **Practice Parallel Walking** — Walk alongside another calm dog at a comfortable distance. This builds positive experiences.
   1. Find a friend with a calm, well-socialized dog
   2. Start walking 20-30 feet apart, same direction
   3. Both dogs should be calm and focused on their handlers
   4. Reward frequently for calm behavior
   5. Over several sessions, gradually decrease distance
   6. Never force direct interaction
   7. This teaches that other dogs aren't scary or over-exciting
4. **Manage Greetings Carefully** — Not all dogs need to greet. But if allowing greetings, do so properly.
   1. Ask the other owner: 'Is your dog friendly?'
   2. Keep leashes loose - tension creates tension
   3. Allow brief sniffing (3-5 seconds)
   4. If either dog shows stress, calmly separate
   5. Signs of stress: stiff body, whale eye, raised hackles, growling
   6. Reward your dog for calm, polite greetings
   7. It's okay to say 'No thanks, we're training' and walk away
5. **Build Confidence Through Other Means** — Confident dogs handle social situations better.
   1. Practice obedience to build confidence
   2. Try confidence-building activities like nose work
   3. Ensure they have positive experiences in many environments
   4. Build a strong bond through play and training
   5. Confident dogs are less likely to react from fear

**What NOT to Do:**
- Forcing interactions with other dogs
- Taking a reactive dog to a dog park - recipe for disaster
- Pulling your dog away suddenly - increases anxiety
- Punishing growling or barking at other dogs - suppresses communication
- Assuming all dogs need to be social butterflies - some are just selective

## Setting Realistic Expectations

- ⏳ **Timeline** — Behavioral change takes 4-12 weeks minimum. Severe issues may take 6-12 months. Progress isn't always linear.
- 📊 **Consistency** — Daily practice is essential. Even one person allowing the old behavior can set you back weeks.
- 🎯 **Professional Help** — Don't hesitate to hire a certified trainer. Some issues require professional guidance for safety and effectiveness.
