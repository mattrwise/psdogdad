# Training Module: Basic Commands

> for safety, communication, and building a strong bond with their dog dad.

## Why These Commands Matter
- 🛡️ **Safety First** — Commands like "Come" and "Leave It" can literally save your dog's life in dangerous situations.
- 💬 **Clear Communication** — Training gives you and your dog a shared language to understand each other's needs.
- 🤝 **Stronger Bond** — Working together builds trust, confidence, and deepens your relationship.

## Sit — Beginner · Typical Timeline: 1-2 weeks

1. **Get Your Dog's Attention** — Start in a quiet space with minimal distractions. Have small, soft training treats ready. Get your dog to focus on you.
   - Use high-value treats like small pieces of chicken or cheese
   - Keep treats in a pouch or pocket for quick access
2. **Lure Into Position** — Hold a treat close to your dog's nose, then slowly move it up and back over their head. As their nose goes up to follow the treat, their bottom will naturally go down.
   - Move the treat slowly - if you go too fast, they'll jump
   - Keep the treat close to their nose throughout the movement
3. **Mark and Reward** — The instant their bottom touches the ground, say 'Yes!' or click a clicker, then immediately give the treat. Timing is crucial!
   - Say your marker word ('Yes!') the moment they sit
   - Give the treat within 1 second of marking
4. **Add the Command** — Once your dog is reliably sitting when you lure them, start saying 'Sit' just before you lure. After 10-20 successful repetitions, try giving the command without the lure.
   - Say 'Sit' clearly and consistently
   - If they don't respond without the lure, go back to luring for a few more sessions
5. **Fade the Lure** — Gradually reduce your hand motion, making it smaller and smaller until you're just giving the verbal command or a small hand signal.
   - Be patient - this can take several days
   - Still reward every successful sit at this stage
6. **Practice Everywhere** — Once mastered at home, practice in the yard, on walks, at the park, and other locations with increasing distractions.
   - Start easy and gradually increase difficulty
   - Always have treats when practicing in new environments

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Repeating the command multiple times - say it once and wait
- Moving the treat too fast causing jumping instead of sitting
- Not rewarding quickly enough - treat must come within 1-2 seconds
- Getting frustrated - keep sessions short (5-10 minutes) and fun

## Stay — Beginner · Typical Timeline: 2-3 weeks

1. **Master Sit First** — Your dog should reliably sit on command before learning stay. Stay is an extension of sit.
   - Don't rush this - a solid sit makes stay much easier
2. **Start Small** — Ask your dog to sit. Put your hand up in a 'stop' gesture and say 'Stay.' Wait just 1-2 seconds, then mark ('Yes!') and reward while they're still sitting.
   - Start with just 1-2 seconds
   - Reward before they break the stay
3. **Add Duration** — Gradually increase the time they stay. Go from 2 seconds to 5, then 10, then 15. If they break, you're going too fast - go back to a shorter time.
   - Increase duration slowly - add 2-3 seconds at a time
   - If they fail 3 times, make it easier
4. **Add Distance** — Once they can stay for 30 seconds, start taking small steps back. Take one step back, return immediately, and reward. Gradually increase distance.
   - Always return to your dog to reward them
   - Don't call them to you - that breaks the stay
5. **Add Distractions** — Practice with toys on the ground, people walking by, or other mild distractions. Start easy and build up.
   - Set your dog up for success
   - Go back to basics if distractions are too much
6. **Release Command** — Always use a release word like 'Okay!' or 'Free!' to tell them the stay is over. This prevents them from deciding when to break.
   - Be consistent with your release word
   - Make the release exciting and reward it

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Moving too fast on duration or distance
- Calling your dog to you instead of returning to reward
- Not using a release word - they need to know when stay is over
- Practicing only at home - must generalize to new locations

## Come (Recall) — Beginner · Typical Timeline: Ongoing - requires continuous reinforcement

1. **Make It the Best Thing Ever** — Coming to you should always result in something amazing - treats, toys, praise, or play. Never call your dog to do something they don't like.
   - Use high-value rewards every single time
   - Never scold after they come to you, even if they did something wrong first
2. **Start Indoors** — Begin in a hallway or small room. Get excited, say your dog's name followed by 'Come!', and run backwards. When they reach you, massive party time!
   - Running away triggers their chase instinct
   - Crouch down to make yourself more inviting
3. **Practice With Distractions** — Practice when they're already coming toward you (mealtime, etc.). Say 'Come!' as they're heading your way, then reward heavily. Build positive associations.
   - Never call them if you're not 90% sure they'll come
   - If unsure, go get them instead
4. **Long Line Training** — Use a 15-30 foot long line in a safe outdoor area. Let them explore, then call 'Come!' If they don't respond, gently reel them in (don't yank). Reward when they reach you.
   - Long lines are available at pet stores
   - Never use retractable leashes for this
5. **Add Real-World Distractions** — Practice at the park, with other people around, with toys visible. Always on a long line until 100% reliable.
   - Don't go off-leash in unfenced areas until rock solid
   - Some dogs may never be reliable off-leash - that's okay
6. **Emergency Recall** — Train a special emergency word (like 'NOW!' or 'TREAT!') that you only use in emergencies. Reserve super high-value rewards (steak, hot dogs) only for this command.
   - Practice this command rarely (once a week max)
   - Make it the most rewarding thing ever

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Calling 'Come' repeatedly without enforcing it
- Using 'Come' for negative things like baths or going in the crate
- Going off-leash too soon in unsafe areas
- Chasing your dog when they don't come - this makes it a game

## Down — Beginner · Typical Timeline: 1-2 weeks

1. **Start From Sit** — Have your dog sit. Hold a treat at their nose, then slowly lower it straight down to the ground between their front paws.
   - Move slowly and steadily
   - Keep the treat close to their nose
2. **Lure to the Ground** — As they follow the treat down, their elbows should touch the ground. The instant they do, mark ('Yes!') and give the treat.
   - Some dogs lie down immediately, others take time
   - If they stand up, start over from sit
3. **Troubleshooting** — If luring straight down doesn't work, try luring down and slightly away from them. This often encourages them to lie down.
   - You can also lure under your bent leg to encourage ducking down
   - Be patient - this can be harder than sit
4. **Add the Command** — Once they're reliably following the lure into a down position, start saying 'Down' before you lure. Gradually fade the lure.
   - Use a calm, lower-pitched voice for 'Down'
   - Practice 5-10 repetitions per session
5. **Build Duration** — Once they'll down on command, work on having them stay in the down position, similar to stay training.
   - Start with just 3-5 seconds
   - Gradually increase time

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Using 'Down' for both lie down and get off furniture (use 'Off' instead)
- Pulling down on the collar - always lure with treats
- Giving up too quickly - some dogs take longer to learn this
- Not differentiating between 'Sit' and 'Down' clearly

## Leave It — Intermediate · Typical Timeline: 2-3 weeks

1. **Start With Closed Fist** — Put a treat in your closed fist. Let your dog sniff, lick, and paw at your hand. Wait. The moment they pull away, even slightly, say 'Yes!' and give them a different treat from your other hand.
   - Don't open your fist if they're still trying
   - Reward from the other hand, not the one they were targeting
2. **Add the Command** — Once they're reliably pulling away from your closed fist, start saying 'Leave it' before showing them the treat. Reward with a different treat when they comply.
   - Say 'Leave it' in a calm, matter-of-fact tone
   - Never give them the treat they were supposed to leave
3. **Open Hand** — Place a treat in your open palm. If they go for it, close your hand. When they pull back, mark and reward from your other hand.
   - Be ready to close your hand quickly
   - This step can take several sessions
4. **On the Ground** — Place a treat on the ground and cover it with your hand. Say 'Leave it.' When they stop trying to get it, mark and reward with a different treat. Gradually remove your hand, being ready to cover it again.
   - Keep your dog on leash for this
   - If they lunge, your hand should already be there
5. **Add Distance** — Place treats or toys at increasing distances. Walk your dog past them on leash. Say 'Leave it' and reward for ignoring the item.
   - Start close to the item and gradually increase distance
   - Always keep them on leash during training
6. **Real World Practice** — Practice with real temptations - food on counters (supervised), interesting items on walks, other dogs' toys at the park.
   - Always supervise early practice
   - This is a lifelong skill that needs regular reinforcement

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Giving them the treat they were supposed to leave - always reward with something else
- Not practicing with real-world items
- Moving too fast through the steps
- Using 'Leave it' after they've already taken something (use 'Drop it' instead)

## Loose Leash Walking — Intermediate · Typical Timeline: 4-6 weeks (ongoing practice)

1. **Choose Your Method** — Decide whether you want your dog on your left or right side (traditionally left). Be consistent. Also decide on acceptable leash tension - no pulling means you feel no pressure on your arm.
   - Front-clip harnesses can help reduce pulling
   - 6-foot leash is ideal for training
2. **Be a Tree** — Start walking. The moment you feel tension on the leash, stop immediately and become completely still. Don't move until the leash goes slack. Then mark ('Yes!') and continue walking.
   - Don't pull back on the leash
   - Be patient - some dogs take 5-10 seconds to figure it out
3. **Change Direction** — When your dog pulls, turn around and walk in the opposite direction. Reward them when they catch up to your side. This teaches them to pay attention to where you're going.
   - Say 'Let's go!' in a happy voice when you turn
   - Don't pull them - just turn and they'll follow
4. **Reward Frequently** — For the first few weeks, reward your dog every few steps when they're walking nicely by your side. Use treats, praise, or both.
   - Treat at your hip level to encourage them to stay close
   - Gradually increase the number of steps between rewards
5. **Practice in Low-Distraction Areas** — Start in your driveway or quiet street before moving to busier areas. Your dog needs to master the basics before adding distractions.
   - Early morning walks can be calmer for practice
   - Keep training walks separate from 'sniffing walks'
6. **Add Distractions Gradually** — Once solid in quiet areas, gradually introduce more distractions - other dogs, people, squirrels. Go back to frequent rewards when adding new distractions.
   - If they're too distracted, you're training in too difficult an environment
   - It's okay to have different expectations for different walks

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Inconsistency - allowing pulling sometimes teaches them to keep trying
- Training walks that are too long - 10-15 minutes is plenty
- Using retractable leashes which teach pulling
- Not rewarding enough in the beginning stages
- Expecting perfect leash manners in highly stimulating environments too soon

## General Training Success Tips

- ⏰ **Keep Sessions Short** — 5-15 minutes is ideal. Multiple short sessions are better than one long one.
- 🎯 **Set Up for Success** — Train in low-distraction environments first, then gradually add challenges.
- 🎉 **Celebrate Small Wins** — Every bit of progress is worth celebrating. Training is a journey, not a race.
- 📅 **Practice Daily** — Consistency is key. Even 5 minutes a day is better than 30 minutes once a week.
- 😊 **Stay Positive** — If you're frustrated, take a break. Your energy directly affects your dog's ability to learn.
- 👥 **Get Everyone Involved** — All family members should use the same commands and techniques for consistency.
